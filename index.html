My Approach

Signal-to-Action Framework

Across product, monetization, and user experience work, I use a consistent analysis loop: detect the signal, localize it, diagnose the mechanism, propose an action, validate the change, and then turn the learning into reusable operating infrastructure.

Adapted from the revenue analytics operating system in my DA playbook, then generalized for broader product analytics work.
Step 01
Signal
Detect anomalies or opportunities in KPI movement: revenue dips, retention loss, toxicity exposure, latency spikes, or balance complaints.
Step 02
Segment
Break the signal down by region, payer tier, user cohort, content type, rank, or device so the problem becomes localizable instead of vague.
Step 03
Diagnose
Use causal logic, funnel decomposition, same-user comparison, or quasi-experimental design to identify the real mechanism rather than surface correlation.
Step 04
Act
Translate findings into product or operations decisions with explicit trade-offs, ownership, and feasible implementation paths.
Step 05
Experiment
Validate recommendations through A/B testing, pre-post tracking, soft launch monitoring, or guardrail metrics before broader rollout.
Step 06
Institutionalize
Turn one-off insight into dashboards, SOPs, threshold rules, and recurring monitoring so the team does not need to rediscover the same answer later.
Project 01

Trust & Safety Governance System

Voice toxicity governance and tiered penalty strategy on a global platform with ~100M DAU

Background

Free Fire is a global tactical shooter with ~100M DAU. Voice toxicity was degrading new player experience, but the organization had conflicting instincts on what to do. Operations wanted aggressive blanket bans, while product leaders worried that many offenders were still valuable users whose removal would damage the ecosystem.

Problem Statement

The core analytical question was not “Can we detect toxic users?” but “What damage is toxicity actually causing to the victim side of the ecosystem?” If that impact could not be quantified correctly, enforcement strategy would be driven by intuition instead of evidence.

Analysis Approach

    >PSM causal inference: Matched toxic-exposed vs non-exposed players on rank, historical KD, daily match count, and registration tenure to estimate the retention effect of exposure. >Survivorship bias diagnosis: Initial matched results showed little retention difference, but this was misleading because only resilient victims stay long enough to report; fragile new users may quit immediately and never appear in the reportable dataset. >Dual-threshold rule design: Proposed a graded enforcement model: ≥99% model confidence allowed auto-penalization, while lower-confidence cases required user reporting to reduce false positives from dialect or joking.

Key Finding

The pivotal insight was that visible victim data was structurally biased. Passive reporting undercounted harm among the most fragile players, so “no significant effect” was itself a sign that the existing measurement system was incomplete. Once the problem was reframed that way, proactive intervention became analytically justified.

Impact & Validation

The dual-threshold policy was used during soft launch and gave the team a balanced operating rule: strict enough to catch silent harm, but conservative enough to avoid over-penalizing ambiguous cases. More importantly, the project created a repeatable governance logic rather than a one-time moderation recommendation.

Why this matters

This case is a strong example of Diagnose over pure modeling. The most valuable output was not a complicated model; it was recognizing that the available data systematically excluded the users most likely to churn.

Project 02

Social Network Health & Retention

Quantifying how network formation affects player retention rather than merely correlates with it

Background

Social features were widely believed to improve retention, but “players with more friends stay longer” is not enough to justify product investment. More social players might simply be the kinds of players who would retain anyway.

Problem Statement

The real question was whether social connection is causal. If retention causes social activity instead of the other way around, then building friend-growth features would have little incremental value.

Analysis Approach

    >Natural variation in network formation: Compared players who formed first connections through different product paths to create more credible variation in timing and intensity of social exposure. >Confounder control: Accounted for skill, tenure, region, and play intensity so that network effects were not just a proxy for “good users.” >Threshold analysis: Measured whether retention gains were linear or whether there was a critical mass effect in active social ties.

Key Finding

Social connection matters most after players pass a threshold of active squad relationships. One or two weak ties add little, but once players have a stable active group, the retention curve meaningfully improves.

Impact & Validation

The product implication was not “maximize raw friend count,” but “help players reach a usable social cluster quickly.” That distinction changed onboarding and recommendation priorities from vanity metrics to retention-relevant network formation.

Why this matters

This is a classic Segment → Diagnose case. Aggregate correlation was easy to observe, but the threshold structure was what made the finding actionable.

Project 03

Combat Balance & Monetization Guardrails

Using same-player comparisons and pre-launch guardrails to protect gameplay fairness while supporting monetization

Background

In a competitive shooter, players are highly sensitive to anything that feels “pay-to-win” or balance-breaking. I worked on two closely related cases: an AUG controversy where players believed the weapon was overtuned, and a Wukong Skill Skin release where the team needed to monetize without breaking combat fairness.

Problem Statement

The challenge was to separate perception from measurable performance impact. For AUG, the question was whether the weapon truly created an unfair combat advantage. For Wukong Skill Skin, the question was how to set pre-launch guardrails so monetization could grow without pushing the character into pay-to-win territory.

Analysis Approach

    >Case A — AUG controversy: Used same-player comparison to reduce player-skill noise, comparing performance when the same users used AUG versus comparable baseline AR choices. This avoided misleading conclusions from raw aggregate KD alone. >Peer benchmarking: Benchmarked AUG against comparable weapons rather than the entire weapon pool, so the team could evaluate whether the observed gap exceeded a reasonable balance tolerance. >Case B — Wukong Skill Skin: Built pre-launch guardrails around combat outcome metrics such as KD / win-rate movement, then monitored soft-launch results against both self-comparison and peer-benchmark baselines. >Monetization-safe design: Treated adoption uplift as a success metric only if combat movement stayed within an acceptable range, so monetization performance could not override fairness constraints.

Key Finding

The AUG analysis showed a meaningful performance gap — about 28% in KD relative to the baseline comparison frame — strong enough to support a targeted balance adjustment rather than dismissing the issue as player sentiment noise. In the Wukong Skill Skin case, the more important result was not just adoption growth, but that the launch stayed inside the guardrail range, which protected trust in combat fairness.

Impact & Validation

These two cases established a reusable template for balance-sensitive monetization work: quantify the gameplay effect using controlled comparison, set acceptable launch ranges before release, and monitor adoption together with fairness guardrails. That made the role of analytics more strategic than “post hoc validation” — it became part of pre-launch decision control.

Why this matters

This project belongs in the portfolio because it shows a different flavor of analytics judgment from the other cases. The hardest part was not finding a significant number; it was designing a comparison frame that players and product teams would both accept as fair.

Project 04

Revenue Decline Diagnosis — Vietnam

Diagnosing a revenue drop in a core market despite stable activity

Background

Vietnam showed a sustained revenue decline over multiple months even though high-level activity stayed relatively stable. That immediately suggested the problem was inside the monetization system rather than at the top of the user funnel.

Problem Statement

If users are still active but revenue is falling, where exactly is the leakage? The team needed to know whether this was macro seasonality, payer-mix degradation, or a content supply issue within the high-value segment.

Analysis Approach

    >Signal decomposition: Split revenue movement into payer behavior components instead of treating “revenue down” as a single undifferentiated problem. >User segmentation: Compared high-pay vs lower-pay cohorts to determine whether the decline was broad-based or concentrated in the users who historically drove monetization. >Content supply diagnosis: Analyzed rerun intensity, ownership rate, and new-content availability to test whether the revenue issue came from saturation rather than weak demand.

Key Finding

The revenue problem was concentrated among high-value users who had already absorbed too much of the existing content pool. Repeated reruns and limited new content meant ownership saturation was high, so the addressable spending opportunity for that segment shrank even while activity held up.

Impact & Validation

The recommendation was to reset content pacing and improve high-value SKU planning rather than blaming market weakness. After execution, new-content ownership among high-value users improved and new-content contribution recovered, which validated that supply quality and novelty were the real operational levers.

Why this matters

This is the cleanest end-to-end framework example in the portfolio: signal detection, payer segmentation, mechanism diagnosis, action design, and then a reusable ownership-rate lens for future monitoring.
