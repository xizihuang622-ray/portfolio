My Approach

Signal-to-Action Framework

Every analysis I do follows a consistent five-step process — from detecting the first anomalous signal to validating the business impact of the action taken.

Step 01
Signal
Detect anomalies or opportunities in metrics — revenue drops, retention dips, engagement shifts
Step 02
Segment
Break the signal down by dimension (region, user tier, content type) to isolate where the problem lives
Step 03
Diagnose
Apply causal methods (PSM, diff-in-diff, funnel analysis) to find the root cause, not just correlation
Step 04
Act
Translate findings into concrete product or operations recommendations with quantified trade-offs
Step 05
Validate
Measure outcome via A/B test or pre/post metrics to confirm impact and close the loop
Project 01

Trust & Safety Governance System

Voice toxicity governance & tiered penalty strategy on a global platform with ~100M DAU

Background

Free Fire is a global tactical shooter with ~100M DAU. Voice toxicity was degrading new player experience. Operations advocated "blanket bans" for toxic players, but Product raised concerns — many reported offenders were high-activity, high-spending users whose removal could hurt platform revenue and community vitality.

Problem Statement

The team had no data to answer: Does voice toxicity actually cause victim churn? Without this, there was no principled basis for choosing between aggressive enforcement and no action. My task was to quantify the causal impact of toxicity on victim retention, then design a governance strategy that could protect the ecosystem without destroying offender LTV.

Analysis Approach

  • PSM Causal Inference: Matched toxic-exposed vs non-exposed players on rank, historical KD, daily match count, and registration tenure to isolate the causal impact of voice toxicity on victim retention.
  • Survivorship Bias Discovery: Initial PSM showed no significant retention difference — but this was misleading. Only "hardcore loyalists" stay long enough to file a report. Fragile new players who quit immediately after one bad experience never appear in the reportable dataset.
  • Dynamic Dual Threshold: Designed a two-tier penalty system: ≥99%+ model confidence → auto-penalize (to capture the invisible victim population); ~80% confidence → require user report (to prevent false positives from dialect or in-game banter).

Key Finding: Survivorship Bias in Victim Data

The most important insight was recognizing that observable victim data suffers from severe survivorship bias. Players who encountered toxicity but stayed long enough to file a report are fundamentally different — more loyal, more invested — than those who quit immediately. This means passive reporting alone systematically undercounts the damage done to the most vulnerable user cohort: new players.

This insight shifted the product conversation from "we don't see a retention problem" to "we can't see the problem using current data pipelines" — and justified the proactive auto-penalization tier.

Impact & Validation

The dual-threshold governance model was adopted by the Trust & Safety operations team. The auto-penalize tier systematically captured toxic events that would previously have gone unreported, while the report-gated tier preserved fairness for dialect/banter edge cases. New player retention in voice-enabled matches improved post-rollout.

Methodology Note

The survivorship bias correction is the analytical core of this project. By recognizing that the "no effect" PSM result was a data artifact — not a true finding — the analysis reframed what problem the product actually needed to solve. This is the Signal → Diagnose step: treating an absence of signal as a signal itself.

Project 02

Social Network Health & Retention

Quantifying the causal effect of social network size on player long-term retention

Background

Free Fire's social features (squad play, friend invites, guild membership) were often cited as retention drivers. The growth team wanted to invest in friend-recommendation features, but product leadership questioned whether social connections actually caused retention, or whether retained players simply happened to be more social.

Problem Statement

Observed correlation between friend count and retention was strong — but was it causal? If high-retention players are naturally more social, then investing in social features would produce no incremental uplift. My task was to separate causation from selection using a natural experiment in the data.

Analysis Approach

  • Natural Experiment Identification: Identified cohorts of players who gained their first friend through different pathways (organic search, system recommendation, guild invite) to create quasi-random variation in network formation timing.
  • Causal Graph Construction: Built a DAG to map confounders — player skill, registration tenure, region, device tier — and applied propensity score matching to isolate the network effect.
  • Threshold Analysis: Found a non-linear relationship: 1–2 friends had minimal impact, but crossing a threshold of 3+ active squad members produced a step-change in 30-day and 90-day retention.

Key Finding: Network Threshold Effect

Social connection is causal for retention — but only beyond a critical mass. Players with 3+ active in-game friends showed materially higher 90-day retention compared to matched peers with fewer connections. Below that threshold, the marginal friend adds limited retention value.

Impact & Validation

The threshold finding directly shaped the friend-recommendation product roadmap — the team redesigned the onboarding flow to target 3 accepted friend requests within the first 7 days, rather than maximizing raw friend count. The finding also informed squad-matching algorithms to prioritize active players over passive contacts.

Methodology Note

The key analytical decision was distinguishing selection (social players retain better by nature) from treatment (social features cause retention). The natural experiment design and causal graph construction are the core of the Diagnose step in this project.

Project 03

In-Game Economy Balance Analysis

Diagnosing currency inflation and its downstream effect on monetization conversion

Background

Free Fire's in-game economy involves multiple currency types (earned and purchased). Over a content release cycle, the team observed that conversion rates on paid content were declining despite stable DAU. The monetization team suspected the economy had become over-generous, reducing the perceived value of paid currency.

Problem Statement

Is the decline in paid conversion driven by economy inflation (too much free currency), by content quality, or by seasonal patterns? Correctly attributing the root cause determines whether the fix is an economy rebalance, a content strategy change, or no action at all.

Analysis Approach

  • Currency Flow Decomposition: Mapped all earn and spend pathways for free currency across the player lifecycle to identify where supply had expanded relative to historical baselines.
  • Conversion Funnel Analysis: Compared paid content conversion rates across cohorts segmented by free-currency balance, to test whether balance level predicted willingness to pay.
  • Content Attribution: Controlled for content release timing and quality scores to isolate economy effects from content effects on conversion.

Key Finding: Economy Saturation in Mid-Tier Players

The conversion decline was concentrated in mid-tier spenders (players who historically made 1–3 paid purchases per month). These players had accumulated free currency through a combination of event rewards that exceeded historical norms — reducing their marginal incentive to purchase. High-spenders and non-spenders showed no significant change.

Impact & Validation

The analysis provided the monetization team with a targeted rebalancing recommendation: reduce free currency earn rate for specific mid-cycle events rather than broad economy adjustments that would affect new player experience. Post-adjustment conversion recovery was tracked via A/B test on the event parameters.

Methodology Note

The segmentation step (breaking the signal by spender tier) was the decisive move in this analysis. The aggregate conversion decline masked a highly localized phenomenon. This illustrates the Signal → Segment → Diagnose chain: what looks like a platform-wide problem is actually a mid-tier player balance problem.

Project 04

Revenue Decline Diagnosis — Vietnam

Diagnosing an 8-month revenue drop in a core market while DAU remained stable

Background

From around August to the following year, Free Fire's Vietnam market showed a sustained revenue decline that was not accompanied by a corresponding drop in DAU or engagement metrics. The business team flagged this as a priority issue: flat user numbers but shrinking monetization implied the problem was internal to the monetization funnel, not a user acquisition or retention failure.

Problem Statement

DAU is stable — so where is the revenue leaking? The gap between user activity and revenue implied a structural change somewhere in the monetization chain: content supply, willingness to pay, or a specific product feature had broken down. My task was to identify the specific mechanism and quantify the contribution of each factor.

Analysis Approach

  • Revenue Decomposition: Broke the revenue metric into its components — DAU × conversion rate × ARPPU — to localize which lever had moved. Conversion rate and ARPPU both declined; DAU was flat. This ruled out a user base explanation.
  • Content Supply Analysis: Tracked the volume and novelty of paid content releases over the 8-month window. Found that new content releases had decreased in frequency and that repeat content accounted for a larger share of revenue-driving bundles.
  • Segment Comparison (VN vs Peer Markets): Compared Vietnam's revenue trend against comparable Southeast Asian markets (same game version, similar user demographics) to control for global product changes. VN underperformed peers, pointing to market-specific content strategy rather than a platform issue.
  • Ownership Rate Tracking: Analyzed ownership rates for featured items to identify content saturation — existing paying users already owned the key items being re-featured, reducing purchase incentive.

Key Finding: Content Saturation in the Core Paying Segment

The revenue decline was primarily driven by content saturation among Vietnam's core paying users. The item ownership rate for re-featured paid content in the paying segment was high — when the same items were re-promoted, the addressable audience of non-owners was too small to generate historical revenue levels. New content contribution to revenue had dropped significantly, and the content pipeline had not been replenished at the pace required to sustain the paying cohort's willingness to spend.

This was not a user behavior change — it was a content supply gap that manifested as a monetization problem.

Impact & Validation

The diagnosis redirected the Vietnam market's content strategy: the content team was asked to increase the cadence of genuinely new paid content (new skins, new bundles) and reduce re-featuring of existing items. Post-adjustment, new content contribution to revenue recovered by over 50%, and overall Vietnam revenue began a recovery trend within two content cycles.

The analysis also surfaced a reusable metric — ownership rate as a leading indicator of content saturation — that was subsequently built into the regional monetization dashboard for ongoing monitoring.

Methodology Note

The revenue decomposition (Signal → Segment) established early that the problem was monetization-structural, not user-behavioral. The peer market comparison (Diagnose) isolated it to Vietnam content strategy. The ownership rate analysis (Diagnose → Act) identified the specific mechanism and pointed to the actionable lever — new content supply. This is a clean end-to-end application of the Signal-to-Action framework.
