Project 01

Trust & Safety Governance System

Voice toxicity governance & tiered penalty strategy on a global platform with ~100M DAU

Background

Free Fire is a global tactical shooter with ~100M DAU. Voice toxicity was degrading new player experience. Operations advocated "blanket bans" for toxic players, but Product discovered many offenders were high-activity, high-spending users. My task: quantify the actual impact and design a governance strategy that protects the ecosystem without destroying core user LTV.

Analysis Approach

  • PSM Causal Inference: Matched toxic-exposed vs non-exposed players on rank, historical KD, daily match count, and registration tenure to estimate the causal impact of toxicity on victim retention.
  • Survivorship Bias Discovery: PSM results showed no significant retention difference between victims and non-victims — but this was misleading. Only "hardcore loyalists" report and stay. Fragile new players who quit after one bad experience never appear in the database.
  • Dynamic Dual Threshold: Designed a two-tier penalty system: >99.5% confidence → auto-penalize (catch invisible victims); ~80% confidence → require user report (prevent false positives from dialect/joking).
  • Server Capacity Planning: Used P99.9 peak concurrent voice sessions (not mean) for infrastructure sizing, saving significant costs.

Key Finding: Survivorship Bias

The most important analytical insight was recognizing that observable victim data suffers from severe survivorship bias. Players who encountered toxicity but stayed long enough to file a report are fundamentally different from those who quit immediately. This meant passive reporting alone cannot capture the full damage.

Methodology Highlight

This project demonstrates the value of causal inference over naive correlation analysis. Surface data suggested "toxicity has no impact on retention" — PSM revealed the survivorship bias and uncovered the true cost of inaction. The dual-threshold design illustrates a key principle in Trust & Safety: precision is the top priority, but recall must be high enough to protect the ecosystem.

Project 02

End-to-End Network Optimization

IDC routing fix & dual-channel networking A/B test

Background

After server consolidation, aggregate metrics showed improved match latency (players entering matches faster), yet community complaints about lag spiked. This is a classic "macro metrics healthy, micro experience collapsed" data trap. My investigation led to two sequential findings.

Figure 2.1: IDC routing distribution (before/after) — 80% of players were incorrectly routed to remote datacenters

A/B Test Design

  • Hypothesis: Enabling dual-channel (WiFi + 4G) for high-ping users significantly reduces severe packet loss.
  • Success Metric: Severe packet loss match ratio
  • Guardrail Metrics: Feature disable rate (< 5%), popup rejection rate (< 10%)
  • Power Analysis: ~14,000 samples per group (α=0.05, Power=0.8, MDE=0.5pp on 2% baseline)
  • Traffic Split: UID last digit hashing, 50/50, 7-day full weekday+weekend cycle
  • Monitoring: Daily chi-square SRM check + Intent-to-Treat (ITT) principle
Figure 2.2: A/B test results — 33% packet loss reduction, all guardrails passed

Results & Impact

Cross-Region Match
90% → <5%
Severe Packet Loss
↓ 30%+ (p<0.001)
Feature Disable Rate
< 2%
User Backlash
None

Methodology Highlight

"Never trust green dashboard metrics" — contradictory signals (fast matching + lag complaints) demand root-cause drilling. Distribution over mean — aggregate latency looked fine, but the cross-sea routing issue was hiding in the tail. Rigorous experiment design with guardrail metrics and ITT principle ensures conclusions survive stakeholder scrutiny.

Project 03

Combat Balance & Monetization Validation

AUG gunplay nerf & Wukong Skill Skin commercialization — balancing fairness and revenue

Case A: AUG Gun Balance

Community complained the new AUG was overpowered. Designers checked overall win rate and deemed it acceptable. I rejected win rate as a single metric (skilled players inflate the average) and introduced a same-player controlled comparison: using SCAR as a baseline anchor, isolating Heroic-tier players, and comparing each player's KD when using AUG vs. SCAR.

Key finding: In Heroic mode, AUG users showed a 28% KD advantage over their own SCAR performance — combat outcomes were determined by gun selection, not player skill. This data directly convinced the design team to nerf.

Figure 3.1: AUG KD gap narrowed from 28% to ~1% after targeted nerf

Case B: Wukong Skill Skin Monetization

After nerfing AUG for being "too strong," launching a paid skin with stat bonuses seemed contradictory. I designed a "dual verification model":

  • Vertical Verification: KD gain ~3-4% (within the 5% perceptibility threshold — historical analysis showed player complaints spike at 8-10% KD differential)
  • Horizontal Verification: Enhanced Wukong still below T0-tier characters — meta ceiling not breached
Figure 3.2: Dual verification — KD gain within safe zone AND below meta ceiling

Results & Impact

28% → ~1%

Methodology Highlight

The "anchor + dual verification" framework is highly transferable: establish a stable baseline (anchor), define a safe fluctuation zone (threshold), use within-subject comparison to eliminate confounding. Applies to e-commerce promotion impact assessment, social algorithm over-intervention evaluation, SaaS pricing strategy — any scenario requiring "is this change safe?" validation.
