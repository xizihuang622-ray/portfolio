My Approach

How I Think About Analysis

Each project follows the same internal logic: locate the real signal, question whether the measurement is trustworthy, then turn the finding into something the team can reuse.

01
Detect the Signal

Start from anomaly detection and progressively localize the issue across dimensions that actually matter — before jumping to solutions.

02
Question the Measurement

Most wrong decisions come from stopping too early — before asking whether the data is actually measuring what it claims to measure.

03
Build Something Reusable

Every non-trivial finding becomes a decision rule, a monitoring threshold, or a documented framework — so the team knows where to look next time.

Four Case Studies

Context

Free Fire’s voice toxicity problem had an obvious surface reading: detect bad actors, penalize them, protect victims. Both sides were already debating enforcement design before anyone had quantified the actual harm.

The real question was earlier: what damage is toxicity actually causing, and can we measure it correctly? If the victim-side pipeline was structurally incomplete, any causal estimate would be biased before analysis even started.

The Pivot

Initial causal matching showed almost no retention difference between exposed and non-exposed victims — which the team was ready to interpret as “toxicity doesn’t cause churn.” But that result was a measurement artifact, not a true finding. Only the most resilient players stay long enough to file a report — users who quit immediately after a toxic encounter never appear in the reportable dataset. “No effect” was a sign the data pipeline was structurally incomplete, not that the problem was minor.

What Changed

Reframing the problem made proactive intervention analytically justified. We designed a graded penalty model: high-confidence detections triggered automatic action — reaching users who would not appear in any report; lower-confidence cases still required user reporting to avoid false positives from dialect or in-game banter.

The more durable output was establishing that passive reporting systematically undercounts harm among new users, and building that corrective logic into the governance design from the start.

Context

After server consolidation, back-end monitoring showed matchmaking speed improved — a surface success. But community reports of lag increased simultaneously. Two contradictory macro signals in the same release window pointed to a measurement mismatch, not an optimization win.

The dashboard looked good because it measured entry speed, not actual match experience. Optimizing the wrong metric produced a misleading positive result.

The Pivot

End-to-end diagnostic from the routing logs revealed that 80%+ of affected users were routed to distant data centers before the fallback threshold was reached. Root cause: an IDC threshold set too conservatively, triggering fallback logic prematurely. After correcting the routing logic with engineering, abnormal cross-region matches dropped from 90% to under 5% and overall latency recovered.

What Changed

After the routing fix, a long-tail segment still experienced poor performance even on local servers. Further breakdown identified the new bottleneck: severe packet loss under weak-network conditions.

We designed a dual-channel network solution and ran an A/B test to measure benefit and side effects. Serious packet loss dropped 30%+; user rejection of the feature prompt stayed within acceptable bounds — confirming safe rollout.

Context

Two cases with the same underlying problem: raw aggregate data was confounded by player-skill selection bias. The AUG looked overpowered in aggregate KD stats, but stronger players gravitate toward stronger weapons — making it impossible to separate weapon effect from player effect without a controlled design.

For the Wukong Skill Skin launch, the question was how to define pre-launch fairness guardrails — so monetization uplift could be validated against pay-to-win thresholds before release, not measured after the fact.

The Pivot

For AUG: same-player controlled comparison (AUG vs. comparable AR baseline, same individuals) removed the skill confound. AUG outperformed baselines by ~28% in KD — large enough to justify a targeted balance change, not dismiss as sentiment noise. For Wukong: monetization success that violates fairness thresholds is not a win — the constraint had to be set before launch as a hard criterion, not evaluated after.

What Changed

These two cases established a reusable pre-launch evaluation template: quantify gameplay impact via controlled comparison, set acceptable outcome ranges before release, and treat fairness guardrails as launch criteria rather than post-hoc audit.

Context

Vietnam showed a multi-month revenue decline while high-level activity stayed stable. The DAU–revenue divergence ruled out an engagement problem and pointed to something structural inside the monetization system.

Revenue decomposition by component and payer-tier segmentation localized where the decline was concentrated — not a broad-based drop, but a collapse in one specific segment.

The Pivot

The problem was not weak demand. It was content saturation in the high-value segment that historically drove most revenue. These users had already owned the majority of items being re-featured. The addressable spending opportunity had shrunk — not because they stopped playing, but because the content supply stopped offering them new things to buy. The fix had to come from the content roadmap, not from discounts or reruns.

What Changed

After content pacing was reset, new-content revenue recovered to ~50% of total market revenue. The ownership-rate metric — tracking saturation of featured items in the paying segment — became a reusable leading indicator built into ongoing market monitoring.
