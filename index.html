My Approach

How I Think About Analysis

I approach product analytics problems with a consistent internal logic: start from anomaly detection, progressively localize the signal across dimensions that actually matter for the business, then invest in diagnosis before jumping to action.

The part I care most about is the step between "we see a signal" and "we know what it means." Most incorrect product decisions I've seen came not from bad data, but from stopping analysis too early — before someone asked whether the data was measuring what it claimed to measure.

Most importantly, I try to turn every non-trivial finding into something the team can reuse — a decision rule, a monitoring threshold, or a documented approach — so the organization gets smarter over time rather than rediscovering the same answer under different circumstances.

The four projects below each illustrate a different phase of that loop, and each centers on one judgment call that wasn't obvious from the first look at the data.

Project 01

Trust & Safety Governance System

Voice toxicity enforcement strategy on a global platform with ~100M DAU

Free Fire's voice toxicity problem had an obvious surface reading: detect bad actors, penalize them, protect victims. The operations team wanted aggressive blanket bans. Product leaders worried about losing high-engagement offenders. The instinct on both sides was to start from enforcement design and work backwards.

The real question I pushed on was earlier: what damage is toxicity actually causing to the victim side, and can we measure it correctly? If we could not answer that, enforcement strategy would be intuition-driven.

The Pivot

Initial causal matching showed almost no retention difference between exposed and non-exposed victims — which the team was ready to interpret as "toxicity doesn't cause churn." But that result was a measurement artifact, not a true finding. Only the most resilient players stay long enough to file a report. The users who quit immediately after a toxic encounter never appear in the reportable dataset at all. "No effect" was a sign that the data pipeline was structurally incomplete, not that the problem was minor.

Reframing the problem this way made proactive intervention analytically justified. We designed a graded penalty model: high-confidence detections triggered automatic action to capture the invisible victim population; lower-confidence cases still required user reporting to avoid false positives from dialect or in-game banter.

The more durable output was not the model — it was establishing that passive reporting systematically undercounts harm among new users, and building that corrective logic into the governance design from the start.

Project 02

Social Network Health & Retention

Whether social features cause retention — or just correlate with it

The growth team wanted to invest in friend-recommendation infrastructure. The underlying assumption was that players with more friends stay longer. That's true observationally, but it conflates two very different things: social connection causing retention, versus retained players simply being more social by nature. If it's the latter, feature investment produces no incremental value.

I looked for natural variation in how players formed their first social connections to get closer to a causal estimate, and tried to understand not just whether social connections matter, but whether the relationship is linear or threshold-based.

The Pivot

The relationship is not linear. One or two weak ties add almost nothing to retention. But once a player crosses into having a stable active squad — roughly 3 or more people they actually play with — the 90-day retention curve shifts meaningfully. The product implication is not "maximize friend count" but "help players reach a usable social cluster quickly." That distinction completely changed what the onboarding redesign should optimize for.

Project 03

Combat Balance & Monetization Guardrails

Separating player perception from measurable performance impact — for both a weapon controversy and a monetization launch

Competitive FPS players are vocal about balance. When they complain that a weapon or character is broken, it could mean many things: the item is genuinely overpowered, or stronger players are simply gravitating toward it, making it look overpowered in aggregate data. These two scenarios require completely different responses.

I worked on two related cases. For the AUG controversy, the question was whether the weapon truly created an unfair combat advantage or whether the raw KD data was just reflecting player-skill selection bias. For the Wukong Skill Skin launch, the question was how to set pre-launch guardrails that would let monetization grow without drifting into pay-to-win territory.

The Pivot

For AUG: comparing the same players' performance when they used AUG versus when they used comparable baseline weapons removed the skill confound. The gap that remained — about 28% in KD — was large enough to justify a targeted balance change rather than dismissing the issue as sentiment noise. For Wukong: the more important outcome was not the adoption uplift, but that the launch stayed inside the guardrail range. Monetization success that violates fairness thresholds is not a win — the framework had to make that constraint explicit before launch, not measure it after.

That shifted analytics from validation work into decision architecture.

Project 04

Revenue Decline Diagnosis — Vietnam

Finding where revenue was leaking when all the top-line activity metrics looked fine

Vietnam showed a multi-month revenue decline while high-level activity stayed relatively stable. That divergence immediately pointed away from a user acquisition or engagement problem and toward something structural inside the monetization system. But "monetization is broken" is not an actionable diagnosis.

The first useful step was decomposing revenue into its components — not treating it as a single number that went down — and then segmenting by payer tier to find where the decline was concentrated.

The Pivot

The problem was not weak demand. It was content saturation in the segment that historically drove most of the market's revenue. High-value users had already owned the majority of items being re-featured in paid content runs. The addressable spending opportunity for that segment had effectively shrunk — not because they stopped playing, but because the content supply stopped giving them new things to buy. The fix was not a promotional push; it was a content pipeline reset.
