My Approach

How I Think About Analysis

I approach product analytics problems with a consistent internal logic: start from anomaly detection, progressively localize the signal across dimensions that actually matter for the business, then invest in diagnosis before jumping to action.

The part I care most about is the step between “we see a signal” and “we know what it means.” Most incorrect product decisions I’ve seen came not from bad data, but from stopping analysis too early — before someone asked whether the data was measuring what it claimed to measure.

Most importantly, I try to turn every non-trivial finding into something the team can reuse — a decision rule, a monitoring threshold, or a documented approach — so the next time a similar signal appears, the team already knows where to look.

The four projects below each illustrate a different phase of that loop, and each centers on one judgment call that wasn’t obvious from the first look at the data.

Free Fire’s voice toxicity problem had an obvious surface reading: detect bad actors, penalize them, protect victims. Both sides were already debating enforcement design before anyone had quantified the actual harm.

The real question I pushed on was earlier: what damage is toxicity actually causing to the victim side, and can we measure it correctly? If we could not answer that, enforcement strategy would be intuition-driven.

The Pivot

Initial causal matching showed almost no retention difference between exposed and non-exposed victims — which the team was ready to interpret as “toxicity doesn’t cause churn.” But that result was a measurement artifact, not a true finding. Only the most resilient players stay long enough to file a report. The users who quit immediately after a toxic encounter never appear in the reportable dataset at all. “No effect” was a sign that the data pipeline was structurally incomplete, not that the problem was minor.

Reframing the problem made proactive intervention analytically justified. We designed a graded penalty model: high-confidence detections triggered automatic action — reaching users who would not otherwise appear in any report; lower-confidence cases still required user reporting to avoid false positives from dialect or in-game banter.

The more durable output was not the model. It was establishing that passive reporting systematically undercounts harm among new users, and building that corrective logic into the governance design from the start.

After a server consolidation, back-end monitoring showed matchmaking speed had improved — on the surface, a successful optimization. But at the same time, community reports of lag and in-game latency increased noticeably. Two signals pointing in opposite directions.

My read was that we had only optimized the entry speed, not the actual match experience. The dashboard looked good because it was measuring the wrong thing.

The Pivot

I ran an end-to-end diagnostic starting from the routing logs. Over 80% of affected users were being routed to distant data centers before the fallback threshold was reached — the root cause was an IDC threshold set too conservatively, triggering the fallback logic too early. After working with engineering to correct the routing logic, abnormal cross-region matches dropped from 90% to under 5% and overall latency recovered.

But after fixing the routing issue, I kept looking. A long-tail segment of users still had poor experience even when connected to local servers. Further analysis identified the new bottleneck: severe packet loss under weak network conditions.

For this segment, we designed a dual-channel network solution and validated it through A/B testing to measure both the benefit and the side effects. The experiment showed serious packet loss dropping 30%+, while user rejection and dismissal of the feature prompt stayed within acceptable bounds — confirming the rollout was safe to scale.

Key takeaway: analysts cannot rely on top-level dashboard metrics alone. When two metrics move in opposite directions, that contradiction is itself a signal worth investigating.

Competitive FPS players are vocal about balance. When they complain that a weapon is broken, it could mean two very different things: the item is genuinely overpowered, or stronger players are simply gravitating toward it, making it look overpowered in aggregate data. These two scenarios require completely different responses.

I worked on two separate but related cases. For the AUG controversy, the question was whether the weapon truly created an unfair combat advantage or whether the raw KD data was just reflecting player-skill selection bias. For the Wukong Skill Skin launch, the question was how to set pre-launch guardrails that would let monetization grow without drifting into pay-to-win territory.

The Pivot

For AUG: comparing the same players’ performance when they used AUG versus comparable baseline weapons removed the skill confound. AUG outperforming comparable AR baselines by about 28% in KD was large enough to justify a targeted balance change rather than dismissing the issue as sentiment noise. For Wukong: the more important outcome was not the adoption uplift, but that the launch stayed inside the guardrail range. Monetization success that violates fairness thresholds is not a win — the constraint had to be made explicit before launch, not measured after.

These two cases established a reusable pre-launch evaluation template: quantify the gameplay impact using controlled comparison, set acceptable outcome ranges before release, and treat fairness guardrails as constraints rather than post-hoc checks. That made analytics part of the launch criteria, not a post-launch debrief.

Vietnam showed a multi-month revenue decline while high-level activity stayed relatively stable. That divergence immediately pointed away from a user acquisition or engagement problem and toward something structural inside the monetization system. But “monetization is broken” is not an actionable diagnosis.

The first useful step was decomposing revenue into its components — not treating it as a single number that went down — and then segmenting by payer tier to find where the decline was concentrated.

The Pivot

The problem was not weak demand. It was content saturation in the segment that historically drove most of the market’s revenue. High-value users had already owned the majority of items being re-featured in paid content runs. The addressable spending opportunity had effectively shrunk — not because they stopped playing, but because the content supply stopped giving them new things to buy. The fix had to come from the content roadmap, not from discounts or rerun promotions.

After the content pacing was reset and new SKU planning improved, new-content revenue contribution recovered to account for roughly half of total revenue in the market. The ownership-rate metric — tracking how saturated the paying segment was on featured items — became a reusable leading indicator built into ongoing market monitoring after this project.
